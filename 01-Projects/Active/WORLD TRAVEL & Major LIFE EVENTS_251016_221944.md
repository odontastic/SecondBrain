people in the Mediterranean had been following for centuries. "The
biggest tenets of that are seafood; lean proteins in the form of beans,

Great news, folks: With the Mediterranean diet, it's a very "you do you"
approach to healthy eating. That being said, the Mediterranean lifestyle
does encourage you

to load your plate with particular foods and food groups.

**Fresh fruits and vegetables:**¬†According to¬†Cleveland Clinic, you
should have at least one serving of veg per meal.

**Seafood:**¬†The Mediterranean diet emphasizes seafood and poultry
protein sources; fish in particular, is a great source of heart-healthy
omega-3s.

**Healthy fats and oils:**¬†Extra-virgin olive oil is touted as the Med
diet's hero, but Berghoff says that other oils, such as walnut and pecan
oil, are also rich in healthy polyphenols.

**Legumes:**¬†Pulses and legumes¬†like lentils, beans, chickpeas, and peas
are great sources of protein, fiber, and healthy fatty acids---and the
Med diet encourages you to eat them multiple times a week.

**Nuts, seeds, and grains:**¬†Whether you're eating 'em as a snack,
sprinkling them on your salads, or making a bowl of whole-grain cereal,
add in nuts.

**Fermented dairy:**¬†While the Med diet recommends reducing your overall
dairy intake to once a day, Feller says that folks in the Mediterranean
reap the gut-healthy benefits of fermented dairy like yogurt and goat
cheese.

According to Berghoff, many of the Mediterranean diet's health benefits
stem from the ways this eating style reduces inflammation within the
body. On the one hand, you're cutting back on foods that are known to
cause inflammation, such as high-sugar foods, highly processed foods,
red meats, and alcohol, she says. On the other, you're increasing the
amount of inflammation-reducing foods you're eating, including plants,
omega-3 fatty acids, and monounsaturated

BOOK SUMMARY - \"Outlive: The Science & Art of Longevity\" by Peter
Attia, M.D.

\- It\'s not about lifespan but HEALTHSPAN, how to live longer and live
better in your later years (the \"Marginal Decade\")

\- The 4 diseases that most people die from or the 4 Horsemen: heart
disease, cancer, neurodegenerative diseases (ie. Alzheimers,
Parkinsons), and metabolic dysfunction (diabetes)

\- The KEY is PREVENTION earlier in life

\- 5 tools for prevention: EXERCISE, nutrition (plant based, nuts, low
carbs, good poly/monounsaturated fats), sleep (6-8 hrs a night),
emotional health, and medicine

I. EXERCISE: aerobic, strength and stability

\- AEROBIC conditioning:

1\) Zone 2 endurance training - four 45-mins sessions a week with steady
pace where you can still talk (ie. treadmill or stationary bike);

2\) VO2 max - hard push/run for 4 mins then jog 4 mins easy where heart
rate drops below 100/min. Repeat this 4-6 times and cool down

\- STRENGTH training: proteins, load-bearing activities, hormone
replacement therapy (HRT), drugs to increase bone density as indicated.
Grip strength; ability to carry heavy stuffs and put them down
(concentric/eccentric loading); pulling motions; hip-hinging movements
(strengthen legs, flutes, lower back)

\- STABILITY training: strong, fluid, flexible, agile. Dynamic
neuromuscular stabilization (DNS) and Postural Restoration Institute
(PRI). Focus on breathing, feet firmly planted on ground, balance,
protect the spine is the goal. Neuromuscular control.

II\. NUTRITION: biochemistry - scientifically rigorous, highly
personalized and driven by

feedback and data. Focus on: nourishment, muscles, metabolic health.
Goal is to reduce energy intake while adding lean mass. So, consume
fewer calories, increase protein intake, pair with proper exercise. 3
methods: caloric restriction (CR), dietary restriction, time restriction
(intermittent fasting). From long-term monkey research, 4 conclusions -
avoiding diabetes and related metabolic dysfunction important to
Longevity, strong link between calories and cancer, the QUALITY of food
is as important as the quantity, if your diet is already high quality
and you are metabolically healthy then only a slight degree of caloric
restriction can still be beneficial. Continuous Glucose Monitoring (CGM)
combined with other biomarkers (lean mass, lipids, uric acid, insulin,
liver enzymes, ApoB and Lp(a) levels). DEXA scan can measure bone
density and muscle mass. For proteins, need 4 servings a day: protein
shake protein snack, 2 protein meals). Protein is very IMPORTANT.
Fats-saturated and poly/monounsaturated are good. Polyunsaturated

fats or PUFA are either omega-6 (corn, soybean) or omega-3s (marine like
fish with EPA/DHA and non-marine like nuts and flaxseed). But, MUFA or
monounsaturated are probably best (olive oil). So, recommended diet:
olive oil, avocado, nuts, salmon, anchovies. Fasting over long periods
forces the body to mobilize fat stores and turns down mTOR, the
pro-growth and pro-aging pathway.

III\. SLEEP: at least 7 1/2 hrs each night. Sleep deprivation can cause
insulin resistance, increase risk of cardiovascular disease, and
impaired cognitive health (risk of Alzheimers and dementia). High stress
level can make us sleep poorly activating the sympathetic response and
metabolic dysfunction. REM sleep is the dream stage and important for
creativity, problem solving, procedural memory, process emotional
memories, maintain emotional awareness. Deep NREM sleep for long-term
memory consolidation in

the hippocampus and brain cleanup (ie. clearing out amyloid beta and
tau, the 2 proteins found in Alzheimers pts). Sleep meds like Ambien or
benzodiazepines induce unconsciousness without improving sleep quality.
Exception is trazodone at low dose can improve sleep architecture (also
the supplement ashwagandha). Best validated sleep questionnaire is the
Pittsburg Sleep Quality Index (also, the Epworth Sleepiness Scale and
the Insomnia Severity Index). Rule out sleep apnea with STOP-BANG
questionnaire or sleep study.

Tips for better sleep: room darkness, limit LED light exposure 2 hrs
before bed, limit cellphone or computer use, cool bedroom (65F), warm
bath before bed, no alcohol or caffeine, exercise during the day, avoid
stressful thoughts, meditation, avoid foods within 3 hrs of bedtime.
Goal is to do whatever increases \"sleep pressure.\"

IV\. EMOTIONAL HEALTH: the way we regulate our emotions and manage
interpersonal

relationships

\- Perhaps the most important of all

\- Some people have emotional trauma as a child. Some of these kids grow
up to become maladaptive, dysfunctional adults: 1) various addictions,
2) co-dependency or excessive psychological reliance on another person,
3) habituated survival strategies (anger, rage), 4) attachment
disorders.

\- Tools: 1) Reframing events and experiences, 2) mindfulness
meditation, 3) dialectical behavior therapy (DBT) to help those who are
suicidal or at risk of self-harm; aimed at breaking the chain reaction
of negative stimulus -\> negative emotion -\> negative thought -\>
negative reaction. DBT has 4 pillars joined by one overarching theme
(mindfulness): emotional regulation, distress tolerance, interpersonal
effectiveness, self management.

\- David Brooks\' \"The Road to Character\" book talks about \"resume
virtues\" like degrees and jobs versus \"eulogy virtues\" (what our
family/friends will remember us by)
